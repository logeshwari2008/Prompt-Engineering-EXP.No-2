Prompt given for zero shot :
       summarise this paragraph into 100 to 150 words
"Blockchain technology, fundamentally, is a decentralized, distributed, and immutable digital ledger designed to record transactions or data across a peer-to-peer network of computers, rather than relying on a single central authority. It is called a "blockchain" because data is structured into, and stored in, individual, encrypted "blocks," which are sequentially linked together to form a "chain". When a new transaction is initiated, it is broadcast to all participants (nodes) in the network, where it is validated through a consensus mechanism, such as Proof of Work (PoW) or Proof of Stake (PoS), ensuring agreement across the network without intermediaries. Once validated, this transaction is added to a new block, which is then sealed with a unique cryptographic signature called a hash. Critically, each block contains not only its own data but also the hash of the previous block, creating a secure, unalterable chain. If any attempt is made to change the data within a past block, the hash changes, breaking the link and alerting the network, thus providing inherent security against tampering. The core benefits of blockchain lie in its transparency—as the ledger is visible to authorized users—and its immutability, which means that once data is recorded, it cannot be changed, deleted, or destroyed. The technology often utilizes public-key cryptography, employing a public key as an address and a private key to authenticate transactions and prove ownership. Beyond the foundational digital ledger, modern blockchains often incorporate smart contracts, which are self-executing programs that automatically carry out agreed-upon terms when specific conditions are met, further reducing the need for trusted third parties. There are4 primary types of blockchain networks: public blockchains (permissionless, like Bitcoin), private blockchains (governed by a single entity), consortium blockchains (governed by a group of organizations), and hybrid blockchains (a mix of private and public). The evolution of blockchain has spanned three main generations: the first focused on cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin (2009), the second introduced smart contracts (Ethereum, 2015), and the third is focused on solving scalability, interoperability, and broader enterprise applications, expanding its impact far beyond finance into supply chain management, healthcare, and digital identity. Ultimately, blockchain technology transforms business processes by providing a single source of truth, increasing efficiency, enhancing security, and enabling traceability."

Data is stored in encrypted blocks that are linked together in a chain using cryptographic hashes, making the system secure and tamper-resistant. Transactions are validated through consensus mechanisms like Proof of Work or Proof of Stake before being added to the blockchain. Once recorded, data cannot be altered, ensuring transparency and immutability. Blockchain uses public-key cryptography for authentication and ownership verification. It also supports smart contracts, which automatically execute agreements when conditions are met. There are four types of blockchains: public, private, consortium, and hybrid. Over time, blockchain has evolved from supporting cryptocurrencies to enabling smart contracts and broader applications in industries such as supply chain, healthcare, and digital identity.

When a new transaction is initiated, it is broadcast to all participants (nodes) in the network, where it is validated through a consensus mechanism, such as Proof of Work (PoW) or Proof of Stake (PoS), ensuring agreement across the network without intermediaries. Once validated, this transaction is added to a new block, which is then sealed with a unique cryptographic signature called a hash. Critically, each block contains not only its own data but also the hash of the previous block, creating a secure, unalterable chain. If any attempt is made to change the data within a past block, the hash changes, breaking the link and alerting the network, thus providing inherent security against tampering. The core benefits of blockchain lie in its transparency—as the ledger is visible to authorized users—and its immutability, which means that once data is recorded, it cannot be changed, deleted, or destroyed. The technology often utilizes public-key cryptography, employing a public key as an address and a private key to authenticate transactions and prove ownership. Beyond the foundational digital ledger, modern blockchains often incorporate smart contracts, which are self-executing programs that automatically carry out agreed-upon terms when specific conditions are met, further reducing the need for trusted third parties. There are4 primary types of blockchain networks: public blockchains (permissionless, like Bitcoin), private blockchains (governed by a single entity), consortium blockchains (governed by a group of organizations), and hybrid blockchains (a mix of private and public). The evolution of blockchain has spanned three main generations: the first focused on cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin (2009), the second introduced smart contracts (Ethereum, 2015), and the third is focused on solving scalability, interoperability, and broader enterprise applications, expanding its impact far beyond finance into supply chain management, healthcare, and digital identity. Ultimately, blockchain technology transforms business processes by providing a single source of truth, increasing efficiency, enhancing security, and enabling traceability."
